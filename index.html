Curator's Note

Where Faith Meets Popular Culture

الدين والثقافة الشعبية

This collection gathers popular media from across the Arab world primarily Egypt and the Gulf states that airs during the month of Ramadan. It spans Ramadan commercials, children's cartoons, sitcoms, Quran recitation competitions, and internet memes.


Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar, observed by over a billion Muslims worldwide through fasting from dawn to sunset, increased prayer, acts of charity, and communal gathering. In Arab countries, it transforms the media landscape entirely: television schedules reorganise around suhoor and iftar times, brands release special seasonal commercials, and entire programming blocks are dedicated to religious and cultural content.


The central question this collection asks is: how does religious life "remix" itself into pop culture and how does pop culture carry religious meaning back into everyday life?

Cultural Practices

Living Traditions of the Month

تقاليد رمضان

"Whoever offers a meal to a fasting person at the time of iftar, he receives as much reward as him, without that detracting from the fasting person's reward."

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ (Hadith)

These traditions are not merely private acts of devotion they spill into streets, television screens, and social media feeds, creating a shared communal experience that defines Ramadan as a deeply social as well as spiritual event.

🍽️
Table of Mercy
مائدة الرحمن
Rooted in the Prophetic hadith about the reward of feeding fasting people, neighbourhoods across Egypt set up communal iftar tables in the streets decorated, open to all, feeding thousands of residents throughout the month. The "Table of Mercy" (Ma'idat al Rahman) transforms ordinary streets into sacred communal dining rooms, blurring the boundary between public space and religious practice. It is one of the most recognisable visual symbols of Egyptian Ramadan.

Qaranqaoh
قرنقعوه
Celebrated on the 15th night of Ramadan in Gulf countries (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, and others), Qaranqaoh marks children completing the first half of the month's fast. Dressed in traditional attire, children go door to door collecting candy and treats as a reward for fasting a near identical structure to Halloween, but rooted in Islamic practice. The tradition powerfully illustrates how religious milestones can generate festive folk culture that travels across generations.
Television & Screen Media

Shows, Cartoons & Commercials

البرامج والإعلانات

Ramadan in the Arab world is the single most watched television season of the year. Networks compete for prestige timeslots, brands invest heavily in seasonal campaigns, and viewers organise their evenings around iftar and the late night Taraweeh prayers. The following are key examples from this collection.

Kids · Channel
MBC3 Ramadan Special Commercial
MBC3, the pan Arab children's channel, produces bespoke Ramadan commercials each year. This example uses bright animation, Arabic nasheed (devotional song), and imagery of lanterns, crescent moons, and family to welcome young viewers into the spirit of the month. It demonstrates how a commercial broadcast network adopts religious iconography to create a sense of seasonal identity religion becomes branding, and branding becomes ritual.

Kids · Puppet Show
Anbar and Lahooh · عنبر ولحوح
A beloved interactive puppet show modelled on Sesame Street, Anbar and Lahooh streams during Ramadan and invites children to phone in to participate in competitions and win prizes. Like its American counterpart, it uses entertainment to transmit values but here, those values are explicitly tied to Islamic practice: patience, generosity, and the significance of fasting. It is a direct "remix" of a secular Western format for a Muslim majority cultural context.

Kids · Cartoon
Muslim Scholars · علماء المسلمين
This animated series commemorates the contributions of Muslim scholars to science, history, and art figures like Ibn Sina, Al Khwarizmi, and Al Biruni. Aired during Ramadan, it offers children a counter narrative to the separation of Islam and intellectual achievement, using the aesthetic language of superhero and adventure cartoons to celebrate historical Muslim thinkers. Religion and academic legacy merge seamlessly in the cartoon format.

Kids · Cartoon
Bakkar · بكار
An Egyptian cartoon series that runs year round but returns to Ramadan themes regularly depicting prayer, fasting, and ablution (wudu) in its episodes as natural parts of everyday Egyptian life. Bakkar's characters live in a neighbourhood that feels familiar to millions of Egyptian children, making religious practice seem ordinary, joyful, and communal rather than formal or distant. It is cultural Islam embedded in slice of life storytelling.

Sitcom · Drama
Ramadan, the One Who Fasts · رمضان أبو صيام
An Egyptian sitcom following a teenage boy named Ramadan through the highs and lows of the month what's permitted while fasting, how to stay accountable, the significance of each practice. By using the coming of age sitcom format (a firmly secular genre), the show makes Islamic religious education engaging for young viewers. It is a striking example of how pop culture genre conventions can carry religious instruction without feeling didactic.
Egypt Watch ↗
Quran · Competition
Crowns of Light · تيجان النور
A Quran recitation competition broadcast from Qatar during Ramadan, featuring reciters from around the world. Segments highlight Quran memorisation centres and programs in Muslim communities globally. The talent show format judges, audiences, elimination rounds is applied to one of Islam's most sacred practices: the oral preservation of the Quran. The result is a powerful fusion of religious tradition and modern television spectacle.

Commercial · Music
Egyptian Ramadan Commercial (2016)
A musical Egyptian commercial from 2016 depicting communal iftars, family gatherings, street decorations, and neighbourhood solidarity. It uses the emotional vocabulary of advertising sweeping music, warm lighting, close ups of faces to evoke the sacred. This is religion remixed through the aesthetics of sentimentality and nostalgia: the commercial sells neither a product nor a service but an emotion that is indistinguishable from religious feeling.

Commercial · Animation
Cartoon Characters Celebrate Ramadan
This commercial deploys popular cartoon characters celebrating Ramadan a direct borrowing of globally recognisable pop culture figures placed into an explicitly Islamic seasonal context. It illustrates the bidirectionality of the remix: not just religious themes entering pop culture, but pop culture characters being absorbed into religious observance. The message for children is clear: your favourite characters fast too.

Film · Cinema
Time Hoppers (2026)
Released in North American cinemas in February 2026, Time Hoppers is an animated film featuring Muslim hero characters travelling through history to meet Muslim scholars. It is the most Western facing item in this collection a deliberate intervention in the superhero movie genre to give Muslim children heroes who reflect their own history and faith. The film brings Islamic intellectual legacy into the Marvel age of popular cinema, asking: who gets to be a hero in pop culture?

Digital Culture

The Meme as Religious Commentary

الميمز والشبكات الاجتماعية

Internet memes have become a primary vehicle for humour, solidarity, and communal self recognition among Muslim communities online. During Ramadan, a specific body of memes circulates around the experience of fasting, praying, and attending Taraweeh the long voluntary night prayers held in mosques throughout the month. These memes are worth taking seriously as texts: they are communally authored, widely shared, and deeply embedded in the lived religious experience of their creators.
